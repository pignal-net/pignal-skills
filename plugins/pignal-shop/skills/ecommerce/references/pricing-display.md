# Pricing Display — Deep Dive

## Pricing Psychology

### Anchoring Effect

The first number a customer sees becomes their mental anchor. Show a higher-priced item first (or a "compare at" price) to make your target price feel reasonable.

- "Compare at $89 — Our price: $49" (the $89 anchor makes $49 feel like a deal)
- Showing the premium tier first in a pricing table anchors perception upward

### Charm Pricing Research

Prices ending in 9 increase demand by an average of 24% (MIT/University of Chicago study). But context matters:
- **Value brands:** $9.99, $29.95 — signals deals and value
- **Premium brands:** $10, $30, $100 — signals quality and confidence
- **Luxury brands:** Round numbers only — $250, not $249.99

### The Decoy Effect

When customers choose between two options, adding a third (strategically priced) option changes their preference:

| Plan | Price | Features |
|------|-------|----------|
| Basic | $10 | 5 items |
| Pro | $25 | 50 items |
| Business | $24 | 20 items ← Decoy |

The Business plan at $24 makes Pro at $25 look like a bargain (50 items for just $1 more). Without the decoy, more people would choose Basic.

## Value Framing Techniques

### Per-Unit Framing
- "$1.50 per cup" is more approachable than "$45 for a 30-pack"
- "Less than $2/day" makes a $50/month subscription feel affordable

### ROI Framing
- "Pays for itself after 3 uses" (compare to the alternative cost)
- "Save $200/year compared to [competitor/alternative]"

### Comparison Framing
- "Less than your daily coffee" (for subscriptions)
- "1/10th the cost of a consultant" (for tools/courses)

## Discount Display

### Percentage vs Absolute
- For items under $100: percentages feel bigger ("Save 25%")
- For items over $100: absolute numbers feel bigger ("Save $50")
- This is called the Rule of 100

### Strike-Through Pricing
Show the original price struck through next to the sale price. The visual contrast emphasizes the savings. But only if the original price was real — fake "was" prices violate trust and often regulations.

### Bundle Pricing
"Buy 2, get 1 free" outperforms "33% off" even though they're mathematically similar. "Free" is more powerful than any discount percentage.

## Subscription vs One-Time

### When to Show Monthly
- When the monthly price is low and the annual total would feel high
- "$9/month" feels more accessible than "$108/year"

### When to Show Annual
- When you want to emphasize savings: "$99/year (save $19 vs monthly)"
- When the annual price competes favorably with alternatives

### Always Show Both
Let the customer choose. Show monthly with a "save X% with annual" nudge. The choice itself increases conversion because the customer feels in control.
