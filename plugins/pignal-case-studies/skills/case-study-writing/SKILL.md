# Case Study Methodology

A case study is proof. Not a claim, not a promise — proof that you can solve a specific type of problem for a specific type of client. The craft is in telling the truth so compellingly that the reader sees their own situation in the story and concludes: these are the people who can help me.

## The Situation-Challenge-Solution-Results Framework

This is the canonical case study structure. It works because it mirrors the reader's decision-making process: "Do they understand my world? Do they understand my problem? Can they solve it? Can they prove it?"

### Situation

Set the scene. Who is the client? What is their industry, size, and context? What were they doing before they encountered the problem?

**Why the situation matters:** The reader is scanning for recognition. "A 50-person SaaS company experiencing rapid growth" — if the reader runs a 50-person SaaS company, they lean in. If the reader runs a 10,000-person enterprise, they might move on. The situation section acts as a filter that connects the right reader to the right case study.

**Principles:**
- Be specific enough to establish relevance without violating confidentiality. "A mid-market B2B SaaS company in the HR technology space" communicates industry, segment, and domain.
- Include the business context, not just the technical context. "The company had just raised a Series B and needed to scale operations to support a 3x increase in customers over 12 months" — this tells the reader the stakes.
- State the status quo. What was working? What was the client already doing? This prevents the case study from implying that the client was incompetent before you arrived.

### Challenge

Define the specific problem or opportunity. What was blocking the client from achieving their goals? What had they tried that did not work?

**Why failed prior attempts are important:** They demonstrate that the problem was genuinely hard. "The client needed a website" is not a case study — it is a service request. "The client had rebuilt their onboarding flow twice in the past year, each time seeing a temporary improvement that reverted within 3 months" — that is a case study, because it shows a problem that resisted obvious solutions.

**Principles:**
- Frame the challenge in business terms, not just technical terms. "High churn rate among enterprise clients" is more compelling to decision-makers than "inconsistent API response times." The technical detail can come in the solution section.
- Quantify the problem. "Customer support tickets had increased 40% quarter-over-quarter" gives the challenge weight. Unquantified challenges feel vague.
- Name the constraints. Budget, timeline, technical limitations, organizational politics — these are the real texture of the challenge and they make the solution more impressive.

### Solution

Describe what you did and why. This section is not a feature list — it is a narrative of problem-solving that demonstrates expertise and judgment.

**Why "what" without "why" is unconvincing:** "We implemented a new dashboard" describes output. "We discovered through user interviews that 80% of support tickets stemmed from three missing data points, so we redesigned the dashboard to surface those points immediately upon login" describes thinking. Decision-makers hire thinking, not output.

**Principles:**
- Lead with the insight, not the deliverable. The insight is what made you choose this solution over alternatives.
- Describe the process in phases. "Discovery and research (2 weeks), design and prototyping (3 weeks), implementation and testing (4 weeks)" gives the reader a sense of rigor and methodology.
- Mention what you chose NOT to do and why. "We considered a complete platform migration but determined that targeted improvements to the existing system would deliver 80% of the value at 20% of the cost and risk" — this demonstrates strategic judgment.
- Be specific about your role versus the client's role. "Our team designed and implemented the integration; the client's engineering team handled the data migration" — clarity about roles builds trust.

### Results

This is the section the reader came for. Everything before this is context for the numbers.

**Principles:**
- Lead with the most impressive metric. "Customer onboarding time dropped from 14 days to 3 days" — if the reader sees this first, they will read the rest.
- Use before/after pairs. "Support tickets: 1,200/month to 340/month" is immediately comprehensible.
- Include timeline. "Within 90 days of launch" grounds the results in reality and prevents the impression of cherry-picked long-term data.
- Separate direct results from secondary effects. "Direct: Onboarding time reduced 78%. Secondary: Support costs decreased $84K/year as a result of fewer onboarding-related tickets."

## Metric Selection

Not all metrics are equally persuasive. The choice of which numbers to highlight is itself a form of argument.

### Leading vs. Lagging Indicators

**Leading indicators** predict future outcomes: user engagement rates, pipeline growth, customer satisfaction scores. They are forward-looking and suggest sustained improvement.

**Lagging indicators** confirm past outcomes: revenue, churn rate, cost savings. They are backward-looking and prove concrete results.

**The strongest case studies include both.** A lagging indicator proves the result happened ("revenue increased 32%"). A leading indicator suggests it will continue ("NPS score improved from 34 to 67, indicating sustained customer satisfaction").

### Avoiding Vanity Metrics

**Vanity metrics** are numbers that look impressive but do not connect to business outcomes. Page views, social media followers, app downloads — these can be gamed, inflated, or meaningless depending on the business model.

**Why vanity metrics undermine case studies:** Sophisticated decision-makers recognize them. A case study that leads with "500,000 page views in the first month" when the client's business model requires purchases, not page views, signals that the author does not understand what matters. Or worse, that they are hiding the real numbers.

**The test for every metric:** Can the reader draw a line from this number to the client's stated business goal? If the challenge was "reduce customer churn" and the result is "increased social media engagement," the connection is missing or fabricated.

### Presenting Numbers Effectively

**Absolute + relative.** "Revenue increased from $2.1M to $2.8M (33% growth)" is more informative than either number alone. The absolute gives scale; the relative gives magnitude.

**Context over raw numbers.** "$700K revenue increase" means different things to a company doing $2M versus one doing $200M. Provide context so the reader can assess significance.

**Round when appropriate.** "Approximately 33% improvement" is more honest and readable than "32.857% improvement." False precision signals insecurity about the results.

## Before/After Framing

The before/after structure is the case study's most persuasive device. It works because the human brain is wired for contrast — change is more visible when the starting point and ending point are presented together.

### Before/After Principles

**Use the same metrics in both.** "Before: 14-day onboarding. After: 3-day onboarding." The metric is identical; only the value changed. Comparing different metrics in before and after ("Before: high churn. After: great NPS score") is not a before/after — it is two disconnected data points.

**Be honest about the baseline.** If the "before" state was not measured precisely, say so. "Before: approximately 1,000 support tickets per month (based on Q3 average)" is more credible than presenting an unqualified number.

**Include the transition.** The most interesting part of a case study is not where the client started or where they ended up — it is how they got from one to the other. The before/after frame is most effective when the solution section clearly connects the two states.

## Client Voice

Direct quotes from the client transform a case study from the consultant's narrative into a shared story. The client's voice adds credibility, personality, and a perspective the author cannot provide.

### Why Client Quotes Work

**Third-party validation.** You saying "the project was successful" is a claim. The client saying "this project changed how we operate" is evidence. Readers instinctively trust the client's assessment more than the consultant's because the client's incentive is to be honest, not to sell.

**Emotional resonance.** Metrics prove results; quotes convey how those results felt. "Our support team went from dreading Monday mornings to actually having time for proactive outreach" — no metric captures this.

### Getting Good Quotes

**Ask open-ended questions.** "What changed for your team after the project?" produces better quotes than "Were you happy with the results?"

**Ask about the journey, not just the outcome.** "What was the moment you knew this was going to work?" produces vivid, specific quotes that bring the case study to life.

**Edit for clarity, not for meaning.** Clean up filler words and verbal tics. Do not rewrite the quote to sound better — that is the consultant's voice, not the client's.

**Always get approval.** Send the final quote to the client for review before publication. This is both ethical and practical — a client who sees an unapproved quote may withdraw permission for the entire case study.

## Methodology Documentation

### Why Process Matters to Buyers

Decision-makers are not just buying results — they are buying a methodology that produces results. A case study that shows impressive outcomes but no methodology leaves the reader wondering: was this luck or skill?

**Document the repeatable parts.** "We conducted 12 user interviews, synthesized findings into a journey map, identified three critical pain points, and prioritized solutions using an impact/effort matrix" — this is a methodology the reader can evaluate and trust.

**Acknowledge the non-repeatable parts.** "The client's engineering team was unusually responsive to feedback, which accelerated the design iteration cycle" — this is honest about what was context-specific.

## Transferable Lessons

The best case studies teach something beyond "hire us." They extract principles that the reader can apply to their own situation, even if they never become a client.

### Why Generosity Builds Trust

**The paradox:** Giving away knowledge in a case study does not reduce the likelihood of being hired — it increases it. A case study that teaches the reader something useful demonstrates expertise more convincingly than one that merely claims it. The reader thinks: "If their free content is this good, their paid work must be exceptional."

**How to extract transferable lessons:**
- "One takeaway: reducing form fields is more impactful than redesigning them. Start by questioning whether each field is necessary before investing in UX improvements."
- "The broader principle: user research should happen before technical decisions, not after."

## Confidentiality

### Working Within Constraints

Not all clients allow public case studies. Some allow anonymized versions. Some allow nothing. Respect these boundaries completely — the trust of existing clients is more valuable than the marketing value of any single case study.

**Anonymization levels:**
1. **Full attribution.** Client name, logo, quotes. The most credible. Only possible with explicit permission.
2. **Disguised.** "A mid-market fintech company." Change identifying details but keep the substance. Many readers will accept this.
3. **Composite.** Combine elements from multiple similar projects into a representative example. Label it clearly as a composite.
4. **Methodology only.** Describe your process and approach without any client-specific details. Useful for demonstrating capability even when case studies are restricted.

## The keySummary

The keySummary is the case study's title. It must communicate the outcome and industry at a glance.

**Good patterns:**
- Outcome + context: "Reducing Customer Onboarding from 14 Days to 3 for a B2B SaaS Platform"
- Challenge + result: "How a Healthcare Startup Cut Support Costs by 72%"
- Client + transformation: "Scaling a Fintech Platform to Handle 10x Transaction Volume"

**Anti-patterns:**
- Vague: "Client Project"
- Internal: "Acme Corp Engagement — Phase 2"
- No outcome: "Website Redesign for Retail Company"
