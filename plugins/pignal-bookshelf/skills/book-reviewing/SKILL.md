# Book Review Craft

This skill covers the domain knowledge of writing useful book reviews -- the structure, critical thinking, and conventions that make reviews genuinely helpful to readers deciding what to read next. It applies when creating or editing book review items on a Pignal bookshelf site via MCP tools.

---

## What a Book Review Is (and Is Not)

A book review is a recommendation engine. Its job is to help a reader decide: **should I spend my time on this book?**

A review is not a book report (plot summary proving you read it). It is not literary criticism (academic analysis of technique and theory). It is not a reaction post ("I cried so hard"). A good review contains elements of all three -- summary, analysis, emotional response -- but organizes them in service of the reader's decision.

---

## Review Structure

### 1. Context

Open by positioning the book. Who wrote it, what kind of book is it, and why does it exist?

- **Genre and category**: "A literary thriller" or "a popular science book about sleep" or "a memoir about immigration." The reader needs to know what shelf this belongs on.
- **Author context**: Relevant previous work, expertise, or perspective. "Sacks was a practicing neurologist for 40 years" matters for understanding why his case studies carry authority. Do not write a biography -- one sentence of relevant context.
- **Comparisons, used carefully**: "If you liked X, this is in the same territory" is helpful. "It's like X meets Y with a dash of Z" is a cliche that communicates nothing. Compare only when the comparison genuinely illuminates.

Why context first? Because a reader who does not read thrillers can stop here. Context acts as a filter, saving the reader time. Launching into plot details before establishing what kind of book this is forces the reader to piece together genre from clues.

### 2. Summary

Describe what the book is about without spoiling it.

**For fiction:** Cover the setup -- the premise, the central character, and the initial situation. Stop before the midpoint turn. A good summary creates enough intrigue to make the reader want to discover the rest themselves. "A detective in 1920s Cairo investigates the disappearance of a British archaeologist, but the case leads her into the city's occult underground" gives premise and stakes without revealing what she finds.

**For nonfiction:** State the book's central argument or thesis, and the type of evidence used. "Kahneman argues that human decision-making relies on two cognitive systems -- fast intuition and slow deliberation -- and demonstrates through decades of behavioral experiments how the fast system leads to predictable errors." The reader now knows the claim and the methodology.

**The spoiler principle:** Reveal only what the author reveals in the first quarter. Anything after that is the reader's to discover. If a twist is central to why the book works, say "there is a mid-book revelation that reframes everything" without saying what it is. The existence of a twist is fair game; its content is not.

Why avoid spoilers so strictly? Because the experience of discovering a story's turns is unrepeatable. A review that spoils robs the reader of something that cannot be returned. If your review requires spoilers to make its point, use a clearly marked spoiler section.

### 3. Analysis

This is the core of the review -- your assessment of how well the book achieves what it sets out to do.

**Evaluate on the book's own terms.** A cozy mystery should not be criticized for lacking literary depth, and a literary novel should not be faulted for slow pacing. The question is: does this book succeed at what it is trying to be? A five-star cozy mystery and a five-star literary novel are both excellent at fundamentally different things.

This is the **taste vs. quality distinction**, and it is the most important concept in reviewing:

- **Quality** is craft: prose clarity, structural coherence, character consistency, factual accuracy, argument rigor. These are somewhat objective. A book with contradictory plot holes has a quality problem regardless of taste.
- **Taste** is preference: preferred genres, tolerance for ambiguity, interest in certain themes, comfort with explicit content. These are personal. A book you disliked because you find stream-of-consciousness exhausting may be excellent stream-of-consciousness.

A good reviewer separates the two. "I don't enjoy military history, but this is an exceptionally well-researched and clearly written account" is an honest, useful review. "This was boring" tells the reader nothing about the book and everything about the reviewer's taste.

**Specific over general.** "The prose is beautiful" is meaningless. "Hamid writes in second person present tense throughout, which creates an unusual intimacy -- you experience the protagonist's displacement as your own" is specific and lets the reader judge for themselves whether that sounds appealing.

**What to analyze:**

For fiction:
- Prose style and voice (concrete examples)
- Character depth and motivation (do they feel like people?)
- Plot structure (satisfying? predictable? deliberately unconventional?)
- Themes (what is the book actually about beneath the plot?)
- Pacing (where does it drag, where does it fly?)

For nonfiction:
- Argument strength (is the thesis well-supported?)
- Evidence quality (anecdotes vs. data vs. expert consensus)
- Accessibility (can a non-expert follow it?)
- Originality (does it say something new, or repackage known ideas?)
- Practical value (can the reader apply what they learn?)

### 4. Recommendation

End with a clear, qualified recommendation. Not just "I liked it" but "who should read this and why."

- **Positive**: "Essential reading for anyone interested in behavioral economics. Start here before Ariely or Thaler."
- **Qualified**: "Rewarding but demanding. If you have patience for dense prose and non-linear timelines, this is extraordinary. If you want a straightforward narrative, look elsewhere."
- **Negative**: "The premise is fascinating but the execution is shallow. For a better treatment of the same topic, try [alternative]."

The qualified recommendation is the most useful kind because it acknowledges that different readers want different things.

## Rating Systems

Ratings are a necessary simplification. They are useful for scanning and sorting a collection, but they are lossy -- a 4-star rating cannot capture why a book is 4 stars. The review text does the real work; the rating is a sorting convenience.

### Common Pitfalls

- **Rating inflation**: If everything is 4 or 5 stars, the rating system carries no information. Use the full range. A 3-star rating means "good, with significant caveats" -- it is not an insult.
- **Rating the experience, not the book**: "I read this during a terrible week so it felt like a slog" is about your week, not the book. Try to separate circumstantial factors from the book's inherent qualities.
- **Different scales for different genres**: Be consistent. If your 5-star thrillers are "unputdownable" and your 5-star literary fiction is "profoundly moving," that is fine -- but a 3-star thriller and a 3-star literary novel should represent comparable levels of "good but flawed" within their respective categories.

### A Useful Scale

- **5**: Exceptional. Changed how I think about something. Would re-read.
- **4**: Very good. Accomplished what it set out to do with skill. Minor flaws.
- **3**: Good. Worthwhile but with notable weaknesses. Glad I read it, would not push it on others.
- **2**: Below average. Interesting premise, poor execution. Finished it but felt my time was poorly spent.
- **1**: Poor. Fundamental problems with craft, accuracy, or coherence. Did not finish, or wished I had not.

## Reading Status Tracking

A bookshelf site is not just reviews -- it is a reading life. Tracking status creates a useful personal record.

### Standard Statuses

- **Want to Read**: The TBR (to-be-read) list. Use tags to note why it is on the list (recommended by whom, heard about where). This context helps prioritize later.
- **Currently Reading**: What is in progress. For a bookshelf site, this might be a private entry with reading notes that evolves into a review.
- **Read**: Finished books. These get full reviews.
- **Abandoned**: Started but did not finish. These deserve a note too -- "stopped at page 80 because..." is useful data for the reader and for anyone considering the book.

**Why track abandoned books?** Because knowing what you do not like is as informative as knowing what you like. A pattern of abandoning books for the same reason (slow pacing, unsympathetic characters, dry academic tone) reveals something about your reading preferences.

## Quote Selection

Good reviews include quotes from the book. Quotes serve as evidence for your claims and give the reader a direct sample of the author's voice.

**Select quotes that are:**
- **Representative**: Typical of the book's style, not an uncharacteristic outlier
- **Self-contained**: Understandable without extensive context
- **Illustrative**: Demonstrating a specific point you make in the review

**Avoid:**
- Quoting the most famous line (the reader has probably already seen it)
- Quoting plot-critical passages (spoiler risk)
- Over-quoting (the review should be your words, not a collage of the author's)

One to three quotes per review is usually sufficient. Frame each quote: explain why you selected it and what it demonstrates.

## Collection Organization

A well-organized bookshelf site makes browsing a pleasure and helps the owner see patterns in their reading life.

### Using Pignal's Taxonomy

- **Item types** = format or broad category: Fiction, Nonfiction, Poetry, Graphic Novel, Short Stories. These are structural and mutually exclusive.
- **Workspaces** = curated shelves: "Best of 2025," "Books About Cities," "Comfort Reads," "Professional Development." These are editorial -- books appear on shelves that the reader curates intentionally.
- **Tags** = descriptive attributes: genre (literary-fiction, sci-fi, memoir, history), theme (identity, technology, nature, grief), mood (funny, dark, meditative, tense), and reading context (book-club, recommended-by-friend, re-read).

### Genre Tags vs. Item Types

Keep item types broad (Fiction/Nonfiction) and use tags for genre specificity (mystery, romance, science, biography). This prevents a proliferation of types while still allowing precise filtering. A reader browsing the site can filter by type "Fiction" then narrow by tag "mystery" to find mystery novels.

## MCP Workflow for Pignal Bookshelf Sites

When operating a Pignal bookshelf site through MCP tools:

1. **Check site metadata first** (`get_metadata`). Understand the configured types, field limits, and existing vocabulary. Bookshelf sites typically distinguish Fiction from Nonfiction at the type level.

2. **Survey the existing collection** (`list_items`). Before adding a review, check what genres, authors, and ratings are already represented. Consistent tagging matters for a browsable collection.

3. **Structure the review content in markdown.** Use the structure above: context paragraph, `## Summary`, `## Analysis`, `## Recommendation`. Include at least one quote using markdown blockquote syntax. The keySummary should be the one-sentence version of your recommendation -- the distilled verdict. "A meticulous, absorbing history of the color blue that somehow illuminates how all cultures construct meaning" tells a potential reader exactly what they need.

4. **Tag consistently.** Before creating new tags, check existing ones with `list_items`. Use lowercase, hyphenated conventions: `literary-fiction` not `Literary Fiction`. Maintain a stable vocabulary so the site's tag-based filtering is actually useful.

5. **Use the title for the book, not the review.** The item title should be the book's title: "Educated" or "Educated by Tara Westover." Not "Why Educated Changed My Mind About Resilience." The review content handles the commentary; the title identifies the book.

6. **Track reading status through visibility and tags.** Books in the "want to read" phase can be `private` entries with a `want-to-read` tag. Finished reviews become `vouched` (public). Books abandoned mid-read get an `abandoned` tag and a note explaining why -- these can stay `private` or go public depending on the owner's preference.

7. **Include book metadata in the content** where the template or type fields do not capture it: author, publication year, page count/length, ISBN if relevant. This aids both the reader of the site and any future structured data extraction.

8. **For re-reads, create a new entry** rather than editing the old one. A second reading of a book is a different experience. Keeping both entries preserves the evolution of the reader's perspective over time.
