# Journaling Craft

This skill covers the domain knowledge of reflective writing -- the practices, structures, and reasoning that make journaling a useful tool for thinking, not just a record of events. It applies when creating or editing journal entries on a Pignal journal site via MCP tools.

---

## Journaling as Practice

The most important principle in journaling is that **consistency matters more than quality.**

A messy paragraph written every day builds a habit, a searchable archive, and incremental self-awareness. A polished essay written once a month does none of these things. The journal is not a publication -- it is a thinking tool. The audience is the future self.

This means: do not edit journal entries after writing them. Do not aim for eloquence. Do not skip a day because you have "nothing interesting" to write about. The boring days are often where patterns become visible in retrospect.

## Entry Structure

Journal entries do not need rigid templates, but a lightweight structure prevents the blank-page problem and ensures entries are useful when revisited.

### The Reflective Framework

The simplest effective structure asks three questions:

1. **What happened?** -- Concrete events, observations, encounters. Not every detail; just what stood out enough to notice. This is the factual anchor.
2. **What did I feel?** -- Emotional response. Name the feeling specifically (see Emotional Vocabulary below). "It was fine" is not reflection. "I felt relieved but also slightly guilty" is.
3. **What did I learn?** -- Insight, shift in perspective, connection to something else. This is where journaling becomes thinking, not just recording.

Why this order matters: starting with facts grounds the entry. Moving to feelings adds the personal dimension that makes journaling different from a logbook. Ending with learning forces synthesis, which is where the actual value of reflection lives.

Not every entry needs all three. A quick daily check-in might only be "What happened?" A processing entry after a difficult event might be entirely "What did I feel?" The framework is a starting point, not a prison.

### Entry Length

There is no correct length. Three sentences can be a complete entry. So can three pages. The goal is to write until the thought is finished, then stop. Padding an entry to hit a word count teaches the writer to fill space, not to think.

For daily practice, most people find 100-300 words sustainable. Enough to reflect meaningfully, short enough to not become a burden.

## Journaling Modes

Different situations call for different approaches. A journal site benefits from variety.

### Daily Reflection

The bread and butter. Write at a consistent time (morning or evening). Cover the day's highlights, lowlights, and one thing learned. The consistency of the ritual matters more than the content of any single entry.

**Morning journaling** (before the day starts) tends toward intention-setting: what do I want to focus on, what am I anxious about, what would make today good? This is closer to planning than reflection.

**Evening journaling** (after the day ends) tends toward processing: what actually happened, how did it differ from expectations, what surprised me? This is reflection proper.

Both are valid. Pick one and do it consistently rather than alternating.

### Gratitude Entries

Gratitude journaling has robust research support for improving wellbeing, but the common advice ("write three things you're grateful for") produces shallow entries that stop working after a week because the writer runs out of novel things to list.

Better approach: **write about one thing in depth.** Why are you grateful for it? What would be different without it? When did you first notice it? Depth over breadth prevents the practice from becoming a mechanical list.

Rotate the lens: gratitude for people, for circumstances, for small sensory pleasures, for difficulties that taught something. The variety keeps the practice from going stale.

### Event-Triggered Entries

Some entries are prompted not by schedule but by experience: a significant conversation, a decision made, a problem solved, a mistake discovered, a moment of unexpected emotion.

These are often the most valuable entries in retrospect because they capture the texture of experience that memory flattens over time. Write them close to the event, while details are fresh. The specific details (what was said, what the room looked like, what you were holding) are what make the entry vivid when revisited months later.

### Weekly Review

A longer entry (weekly or fortnightly) that steps back from daily detail:

- **Themes**: What kept coming up this week?
- **Progress**: What moved forward? What is stuck?
- **Energy**: What gave me energy? What drained it?
- **Adjustment**: What do I want to do differently next week?

The weekly review is where patterns across daily entries become visible. It is meta-journaling -- reflecting on the reflections.

## Prompt Techniques

When the writer does not know what to write, prompts break the inertia. Good prompts are open-ended but specific enough to spark a concrete memory or opinion.

### Effective Prompt Categories

- **Observational**: "Describe one thing you noticed today that you normally overlook." Forces present-moment attention.
- **Relational**: "Write about a conversation that stayed with you this week." Surfaces interpersonal patterns.
- **Counterfactual**: "What would you do differently if you could redo today?" Builds decision-awareness.
- **Sensory**: "Describe a meal, a sound, a texture from today." Grounds abstract reflection in physical experience.
- **Values**: "When did your actions align with what matters to you? When didn't they?" Connects behavior to identity.

### Prompts to Avoid

- Overly broad: "What do you think about life?" (too abstract, produces platitudes)
- Leading: "What are you most proud of?" (assumes pride is present, creates pressure)
- Binary: "Did you have a good day?" (yes/no does not produce reflection)

## Emotional Vocabulary

"Fine," "good," "bad," and "stressed" are not emotions -- they are summaries that collapse complex internal states into useless labels. Specific emotional vocabulary makes journal entries genuinely useful for self-understanding.

Why this matters: the act of naming an emotion precisely changes the relationship to it. "I'm stressed" gives you nothing to work with. "I feel overwhelmed by the number of decisions I need to make, and underneath that is fear that I'll choose wrong" is specific enough to act on.

### Building Vocabulary

Instead of **happy**, try: content, relieved, proud, grateful, excited, amused, peaceful, hopeful, delighted, satisfied.

Instead of **sad**, try: disappointed, grieving, lonely, nostalgic, melancholy, discouraged, helpless, empty, homesick, regretful.

Instead of **angry**, try: frustrated, resentful, betrayed, irritated, indignant, defensive, impatient, jealous, contemptuous, violated.

Instead of **anxious**, try: apprehensive, uncertain, vulnerable, overwhelmed, restless, hypervigilant, dread, self-conscious, insecure, exposed.

Instead of **good**, try: energized, connected, accomplished, creative, focused, at ease, confident, curious, inspired, alive.

The point is not to perform emotional complexity but to distinguish between states that feel similar on the surface but have different causes and different implications.

## Privacy and Visibility

Journals are private by default. This is a fundamental principle, not a suggestion.

**Why private-by-default matters:** The value of journaling comes from honesty, and honesty requires safety. If the writer is performing for an audience -- even a hypothetical future audience -- they self-censor. Self-censorship defeats the purpose.

On a Pignal journal site:

- **Private** (`private` visibility): The default for all entries. Only the site owner sees them. This is where raw, unfiltered reflection happens.
- **Unlisted** (`unlisted` visibility): Shareable via direct link. Useful for sharing a specific entry with a therapist, friend, or accountability partner without making it publicly discoverable.
- **Public** (`vouched` visibility): Rare for journal entries. Appropriate for polished reflections the writer intentionally wants to publish -- more like personal essays than diary entries. The writer should make this choice deliberately, not by default.

When helping a user with their journal site, never suggest making entries public unless they explicitly want to. Assume private.

## Bullet Journaling for Digital

The analog bullet journal system (rapid logging with symbols) adapts well to a digital journal site when simplified:

- **Tasks** marked with a checkbox or dash
- **Events** marked as facts (date, what happened)
- **Notes** as observations or ideas

Digital advantage over paper: searchability. Tag entries consistently so past reflections are findable. A paper journal requires flipping pages; a Pignal journal with good tags lets you find every entry about, say, a specific project or relationship.

Digital disadvantage: the temptation to edit. Resist it. The journal records the state of mind at the time of writing. Editing retroactively falsifies the record and defeats the purpose.

## MCP Workflow for Pignal Journal Sites

When operating a Pignal journal site through MCP tools:

1. **Check site metadata first** (`get_metadata`). Understand the configured item types and vocabulary. Journal sites might have types like "Daily," "Weekly Review," "Gratitude," "Reflection," or might use a single type.

2. **Respect the privacy model.** Create entries as `private` unless the user explicitly asks otherwise. Do not suggest publishing journal entries. The user decides what, if anything, becomes public.

3. **Use the keySummary field as a one-line essence.** For a journal entry, the keySummary should capture the emotional core or key insight, not a factual summary. "Realized I keep saying yes to things out of guilt, not generosity" is a useful keySummary. "Went to work, had meetings, came home" is not.

4. **Tag for future retrieval, not for display.** Tags on a journal are primarily for the writer's own search. Use consistent conventions: mood tags (reflective, anxious, energized, grateful), topic tags (work, relationships, health, creative), type tags (daily, weekly-review, milestone). Check existing tags with `list_items` before inventing new ones.

5. **Date-based titling is fine for journals.** Unlike recipes or book reviews, journal entries do not need descriptive titles. "2026-03-27" or "Thursday Morning" or "After the Meeting" are all appropriate. The content carries the meaning, not the title.

6. **When prompted to help with journaling**, use the reflective framework (What happened? What did I feel? What did I learn?) as a conversation guide. Ask follow-up questions that deepen reflection rather than broaden it. "You said you felt frustrated -- what specifically triggered that?" is better than "What else happened today?"

7. **Use workspaces for life areas** when the journal grows large. Workspaces like "Work," "Personal," "Creative Projects," or "Health" help the writer review specific threads of their life without wading through everything.
